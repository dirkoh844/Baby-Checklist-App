Settle these five before labor

Everything else the hospital will either agree to or decide in the room. These five change the outcome only if you handle them now, at a prenatal visit.

  1. The Pitocin position. Decide what happens if labor arrests, before it does.
  2. The two legal citations. Both are wrong for what they are cited for (see sections 0 and 3).
  3. Husband in the OR during spinal placement. An anesthesia decision, and the likeliest refusal.
  4. Wireless monitoring. Confirm the unit has it and reserve one.
  5. The HBsAg result. In hand at admission, and the Hep B question answers itself.

0

Before labor and at admission

Routine here

"These preferences are the conditional terms of my consent — for me and my baby. In any non-emergency, please obtain explicit verbal consent before any intervention, medication, or procedure. Please discuss alternatives first."

Why, for the doctor: Informed consent is the legal baseline, not a favor. Federal Conditions of Participation (42 CFR 482.13) give a patient the right to participate in care planning and to refuse treatment. Framing preferences as the terms of consent is simply how consent works, and stating it up front saves the team from guessing at 3 a.m.

Settle prenatally

"Provided under VA Health Care Decisions Act and 12VAC5-20-100. Refusals require explicit verbal authorization to override outside of true emergency."

Why, for the doctor: The Virginia Health Care Decisions Act (Va. Code 54.1-2981 et seq.) is solid ground for informed refusal.

Heads up: Fix this citation before you print. 12VAC5-20-100 is the informed-consent section of Virginia's Regulations for the Conduct of Human Research. It governs research subjects, not maternity care. A risk-management nurse who looks it up can use it to wave off the whole legal framing. Drop it, or replace it with 42 CFR 482.13 and Sentara's own Patient Rights.

Routine here

"On file in MyChart."

Why, for the doctor: The night nurse reads the chart, not the paper in your bag. Ask that it be attached where the L&D summary shows it, not buried in scanned documents, and bring two printed copies anyway: one for the nurse, one taped where the team can see it.

Usually yes — ask

"No medical, nursing, or midwifery students. Introduce every caregiver by name and role (per Sentara Patient Rights). Residents per OB-practice arrangement — verbal consent before each exam or procedure."

Why, for the doctor: Sentara Norfolk General is a teaching hospital. Declining students is routinely honored and costs the unit nothing. Residents are different: they are part of the practice's coverage, so expect them at the bedside. Asking for consent before each resident exam is reasonable and matches Sentara's own patient rights language. Ask the OB now which residents cover the practice.

Usually yes — ask

"Saline lock — no continuous IV fluids. 18-gauge, non-dominant forearm. Hydrating by mouth."

Why, for the doctor: For any trial of labor after cesarean the unit wants reliable large-bore access in case of an emergent cesarean or hemorrhage. An 18-gauge saline lock gives them exactly that while leaving her free to walk, so this is an easy yes. Oral hydration in labor is appropriate for low-risk patients.

Heads up: Two things reverse it: an epidural needs a fluid bolus first, and if oxytocin is ever accepted it runs in fluids. Expect "no continuous IV" to bend if either happens.

1

During labor

Usually yes — ask

"Continuous wireless EFM — preserves mobility. No scalp electrode unless external uninterpretable."

Why, for the doctor: This is the single most important VBAC safety measure, and she is asking for it, not declining it. An abnormal fetal heart rate tracing is the most common and usually the earliest sign of uterine rupture, so continuous monitoring is the thing that catches a rupture in time. She is only asking for the wireless version so monitoring and movement can coexist. Her condition on the scalp electrode (only when the external trace cannot be read) is the standard indication anyway, so there is nothing to argue about.

Heads up: Telemetry units are limited and get claimed on a busy floor. Confirm SNGH has wireless monitoring and ask them to reserve one at admission.

Routine here

"Freedom of movement. Walking, birth ball, peanut ball, squat bar, any position welcome."

Why, for the doctor: Upright and mobile positions in the first stage shorten labor and are a first-line comfort measure. Fully compatible with wireless monitoring. Ask for the squat bar to be in the room before pushing rather than hunted down during it.

Routine here

"Minimize cervical exams. Ask before each one."

Why, for the doctor: Every exam after the membranes rupture adds infection risk, and no exam predicts when birth will happen. She is not refusing them, only asking that each one have a reason and her consent. Easy yes.

Usually yes — ask

"No artificial rupture of membranes (AROM). Membranes to rupture on their own."

Why, for the doctor: AROM shortens labor modestly at best, and it starts an infection clock that increases the pressure toward augmentation and a delivery deadline. Declining it is low risk and reasonable.

Heads up: If labor stalls, AROM is usually the first thing offered as the alternative to Pitocin. If both are declined, the remaining options are time or a cesarean. Decide in advance which of the two you would rather accept, because you will be asked in the moment.

FIRM — VBAC SAFETY Three yeses, one fight

"No Pitocin (induction or augmentation). No misoprostol (Cytotec). No prostaglandins (Cervidil / Prepidil). Induction, if necessary: mechanical ripening (Foley / Cook) only."

Why, for the doctor: This block is three easy wins and one real fight, so split it when you talk to the OB.

Misoprostol (Cytotec)
Routine here		ACOG agrees with her. ACOG states misoprostol should not be used for cervical ripening or induction at term in patients with a prior cesarean (Level A). A randomized trial of misoprostol for TOLAC induction was stopped early for safety after ruptures. Her refusal is the guideline.
Prostaglandins (Cervidil / Prepidil)
Routine here		Evidence is inconsistent and several large studies show a higher rupture rate with prostaglandin induction after a cesarean; ACOG declines to make a definitive recommendation. Refusing is comfortably within the evidence, and most units avoid them in a TOLAC anyway.
Mechanical (Foley / Cook)
Routine here		This is the right ask. ACOG notes no compelling evidence of increased rupture risk with transcervical catheters and says they may be an option for TOLAC candidates with an unfavorable cervix. Naming the alternative you will accept is what makes a refusal easy to say yes to.
Pitocin (oxytocin)
Expect pushback		Here is the fight. Oxytocin remains the mainstay both for induction and for treating a stalled labor in TOLAC, and ACOG does not prohibit it. The risk looks dose-dependent rather than all-or-nothing: a 2025 meta-analysis found higher rupture risk with escalation intervals shorter than 30 minutes and maximum doses above 20 mU/min. Expect the OB to push, and expect it hardest at the moment labor arrests.

Heads up: Know the trade-off before you are 8 cm and exhausted. If labor arrests and both Pitocin and AROM are refused, the realistic path is a repeat cesarean. A middle position worth raising with the OB now, entirely her call: accept augmentation only under a low-dose protocol, escalation no more often than every 30 minutes, a ceiling near 20 mU/min, and only for a defined arrest. That keeps the VBAC alive while staying in the safest part of the dose-response curve.

Routine here

"Epidural decision pending. Offer non-pharmacologic first (position, shower, breathing, counter-pressure) and nitrous oxide if available. Do not offer epidural — I will ask."

Why, for the doctor: "Do not offer" is an easy request for a nurse to honor; put it on the whiteboard. Worth telling the team what is not being said here: an epidural does not mask uterine rupture, and ACOG considers epidural analgesia acceptable during a trial of labor, partly because good pain control helps a woman keep going. If she asks for one at hour 20, that is not a VBAC safety failure.

Heads up: Nitrous oxide availability varies by unit and is not guaranteed. Ask on the tour, not in triage.

Signs of uterine rupture we are watching for Routine here

"Severe persistent scar pain, sudden change in pain quality, hematuria, recession of presenting part, abnormal FHR tracing, maternal tachycardia, loss of contraction pattern, sudden vaginal bleeding. We will alert nursing immediately."

Why, for the doctor: This list is the most persuasive thing in the whole document. It tells the team she understands the risk she is accepting and that she is a partner in catching it, which is exactly what makes the rest of the plan credible rather than adversarial. The earliest sign is usually the tracing, which is the argument for the wireless monitor. Ask the nurse to tell you promptly if the tracing changes.

2

Delivery and pushing

Routine here

"Spontaneous, open-glottis pushing on my urge and timing. No directed counting. Any position as tracing permits."

Why, for the doctor: Directed Valsalva counting has no proven benefit; spontaneous pushing performs at least as well, with less perineal trauma and better fetal oxygenation. Her own clause, "as tracing permits," is what makes this an easy yes: she has already conceded the safety condition.

Heads up: With an epidural, position options narrow to what she can hold safely.

FIRM Routine here

"No episiotomy. Perineal support and warm compresses preferred. I accept natural tearing."

Why, for the doctor: ACOG recommends restricted rather than routine episiotomy, and routine cutting increases severe perineal injury. Warm compresses on the perineum in the second stage reduce third- and fourth-degree tears. She is asking for current best practice, so this should be a yes without discussion.

Heads up: A shoulder dystocia or an operative (vacuum or forceps) delivery can create a genuine indication. Decide whether you want an explicit carve-out in the text, or you are content to rely on the emergency clause at the top.

Routine here

"Delayed cord clamping >=60 sec, or until cord stops pulsing (per ACOG)."

Why, for the doctor: ACOG recommends delaying clamping at least 30 to 60 seconds in vigorous term infants; it raises the newborn's iron stores. This is already standard practice, and her citation is correct.

Routine here

"Cord cutting: my husband."

Why, for the doctor: Routine, and usually offered without being asked.

3

Immediately after birth — baby

Routine here

"Immediate, uninterrupted skin-to-skin — drying and APGAR on my chest. No separation."

Why, for the doctor: For a vigorous newborn, drying, stimulation, and APGAR scoring are all done on the chest; nothing is lost clinically. Skin-to-skin stabilizes temperature, blood sugar, and heart rate and improves breastfeeding. Standard at any center doing family-centered care.

Heads up: A baby who needs resuscitation goes to the warmer. That is the emergency clause working as intended, not a broken promise.

Routine here

"Protect the golden hour — defer weighing, measuring, and non-essential procedures 60+ min."

Why, for the doctor: Nothing in the newborn assessment is time-critical for a stable baby. Weights and measurements keep. Say it once at delivery and again at the shift change, because the second nurse did not hear the first conversation.

FIRM Expect pushback

"No erythromycin eye ointment. Prepare refusal form (VA law 12VAC5-90-140)."

Why, for the doctor: Understand why they will not drop this casually: the ointment prevents gonococcal ophthalmia neonatorum, which can scar the cornea and blind a newborn within roughly 24 to 48 hours. The USPSTF grades universal prophylaxis A. Your strongest argument is the one that is missing from the document: a negative prenatal gonorrhea and chlamydia screen. With a negative maternal screen, the infection this prevents is effectively off the table. Bring the lab result, do not just assert it.

Heads up: Second citation problem. 12VAC5-90-140 is the regulation that requires the clinician to place the ointment. It is a duty on the provider, not a parental opt-out, and it contains no refusal mechanism. In practice, a documented parental refusal is accepted and Sentara will hand you a declination form to sign. But citing this rule as your authority to refuse invites an immediate correction. Rest the refusal on informed consent and the negative screen instead.

Routine here

"Delay first bath 24+ hours, or decline entirely (preserving vernix)."

Why, for the doctor: WHO recommends delaying the first bath at least 24 hours; delayed bathing improves temperature and blood-sugar stability and breastfeeding rates. Many US units already default to 12 to 24 hours, so this is often already their policy.

Routine here

"Baby stays with me — all assessments in room. Husband accompanies baby if transported."

Why, for the doctor: Couplet care is the standard model; the exam comes to the room. The second sentence is the one that matters, so say it out loud at the moment anyone picks the baby up.

Usually yes — ask

"Exclusive breastfeeding. No pacifiers, bottles, or formula without my consent. Lactation consult within 24 hrs."

Why, for the doctor: Standard in any Baby-Friendly-aligned unit, and lactation consults are usually available same day. Ask for the consult to be ordered at admission rather than requested afterward.

Heads up: If the baby has documented low blood sugar or excessive weight loss, they will recommend supplementation, and they should. Consent is still yours. Decide now whether your first choice would be expressed colostrum, donor milk, or formula, so the answer is ready.

Newborn procedures — pending decision

Settle prenatally

"Hepatitis B vaccine (birth dose): pending — confirming HBsAg-negative status. Explicit consent required before administration."

Why, for the doctor: Expect a firm recommendation. AAP and CDC advise the birth dose within 24 hours for every medically stable newborn regardless of maternal status, because it is a safety net against unknown status, lab error, and household exposure. Your position is a deferral tied to a lab result rather than a refusal, which is defensible and easy to explain.

Heads up: Have the HBsAg result in hand at admission and the question resolves itself. Otherwise expect a deferral or declination form, and expect the pediatric team to raise it again at discharge.

5

If a repeat cesarean becomes necessary

Routine here

"True emergency: proceed without delay. We trust you."

Why, for the doctor: Do not underestimate this line. It is the reason the team will take the rest of the document seriously instead of filing it as a fight. Keep it at the top of the page and say it out loud on admission.

Expect pushback

"My husband present in OR throughout — including during spinal / epidural placement."

Why, for the doctor: This one is an anesthesia decision, not the OB's, and it is the item most likely to be refused. Many units keep the partner outside until the block is placed and the drape is up, citing sterile field and the anesthesiologist's need for an unobstructed patient.

Heads up: Settle this prenatally with the anesthesia group, not on the day. Ask for the answer to be documented in the chart. If they will not agree, know it in advance rather than discovering it at the OR door.

Usually yes — ask

"Clear drape preferred — I want to see baby being born."

Why, for the doctor: Increasingly common and usually granted when requested ahead of time. The catch is stock: many ORs do not keep clear drapes. Ask whether SNGH has them, and whether you may bring your own if not.

Usually yes — ask

"Delayed cord clamping >=60 sec if clinically possible."

Why, for the doctor: Often possible in a non-emergent repeat cesarean, and her qualifier ("if clinically possible") already concedes the exception. Easy yes.

Usually yes — ask

"Immediate skin-to-skin in OR (husband if I cannot), continued in recovery. No nursery. Husband accompanies baby if separated."

Why, for the doctor: This is a family-centered cesarean, and it is a staffing question more than a policy one: it needs a nurse assigned to the baby at the mother's chest. Centers that do it routinely need to know in advance to staff it.

Heads up: Confirm prenatally that SNGH does skin-to-skin in the OR and put it in the chart. Asking during a cesarean is too late to arrange a person.
