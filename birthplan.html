Settle these five before labor

These choices depend most on individual VBAC history, hospital capability, and current clinical guidance. Discuss them at a prenatal visit while there is time to understand options and document a fallback.

The five: Pitocin, citations, OR access, monitoring, HBsAg
  1. The Pitocin position. Decide what happens if labor arrests, before it does.
  2. Consent and refusal wording. Keep it plain; review the hospital's patient-rights policy rather than presenting this draft as legal advice.
  3. Support person in the OR during spinal placement. An anesthesia decision, and a likely point of discussion.
  4. Wireless monitoring. Confirm the unit has it and reserve one.
  5. The HBsAg result and Hep B plan. Bring the result and discuss the current AAP and CDC recommendations with the pediatrician.

0Before labor and at admission5 preferences
Often routine

"For non-emergency care, please explain the recommendation, material benefits and risks, and reasonable alternatives, then ask for my consent before an intervention, medication, or procedure."

Why / what to discuss

Federal hospital patient-rights rules address participation in care planning and informed decisions. Plain, collaborative wording is easier to use at the bedside than a legal conclusion; ask the hospital how it documents consent and refusal.

Usually yes — ask

"If I decline a non-emergency recommendation after discussion, please document the conversation and my decision. If circumstances change, explain what changed and ask me again."

Why / what to discuss

Documentation gives the next shift an accurate record and leaves room to revisit a decision if the facts change. The prior draft cited 12VAC5-20-100, but that section governs human-subject research and is not authority for routine maternity care, so it has been removed.

Often routine

"On file in MyChart."

Why / what to discuss

An electronic-chart copy helps each shift find the preferences. Ask where patient preferences are displayed in the hospital's record, and bring two printed copies as a practical backup.

Usually yes — ask

"No medical, nursing, or midwifery students. Please introduce every caregiver by name and role. Residents per the OB-practice arrangement; ask before each exam or procedure."

Why / what to discuss

Teaching roles and consent processes vary by hospital and practice. Ask prenatally who may participate, which clinicians are part of the covering team, and how a preference to decline student involvement is recorded.

Usually yes — ask

"Saline lock — no continuous IV fluids. 18-gauge, non-dominant forearm. Hydrating by mouth."

Why / what to discuss

IV-access expectations during TOLAC vary by unit and individual risk. A saline lock can preserve mobility while keeping access available, but the team should choose placement and gauge. ACOG supports moderate amounts of clear liquids for many uncomplicated labors; anesthesia, medication, or clinical concerns can change that plan.

Heads up: Epidural, oxytocin, dehydration, bleeding, or another clinical change may require fluids or different access. Ask what would trigger the change.

Usually yes — ask

"Continuous wireless EFM — preserves mobility. No scalp electrode unless external uninterpretable."

Why / what to discuss

Continuous fetal-heart-rate monitoring is commonly recommended during TOLAC because tracing changes can be a sign of uterine rupture or another problem. Wireless telemetry can preserve mobility when the unit has reliable equipment. Ask how the team handles an unreadable external tracing and when an internal monitor would be considered.

Heads up: Wireless telemetry availability varies and units may be limited. Confirm the hospital's equipment and process before labor; have a mobility plan for wired monitoring too.

Often routine

"Freedom of movement. Walking, birth ball, peanut ball, squat bar, any position welcome."

Why / what to discuss

Upright and mobile positions in the first stage shorten labor and are a first-line comfort measure. Fully compatible with wireless monitoring. Ask for the squat bar to be in the room before pushing rather than hunted down during it.

Often routine

"Minimize cervical exams. Ask before each one."

Why / what to discuss

Repeated cervical exams after membranes rupture can contribute to infection risk. Exams can still provide clinically useful information, so ask what question each exam will answer and consent to it in context.

Usually yes — ask

"No artificial rupture of membranes (AROM). Membranes to rupture on their own."

Why / what to discuss

Artificial rupture can sometimes be used to augment labor, but its benefit and risks depend on dilation, fetal position, infection context, and the rest of the plan. Ask the clinician what problem it is intended to solve and what alternatives are reasonable at that moment.

Heads up: If labor stalls and both AROM and oxytocin are declined, continued observation may or may not remain safe, and a repeat cesarean may become the recommended fallback. Discuss that pathway before labor.

FIRM — VBAC SAFETY Separate decisions

"No Pitocin (induction or augmentation). No misoprostol (Cytotec). No prostaglandins (Cervidil / Prepidil). Induction, if necessary: mechanical ripening (Foley / Cook) only."

Why / what to discuss

These methods do not have identical evidence or risk. Review them one at a time with the obstetric team and tie the final plan to the prior uterine incision, reason for induction, cervical exam, and hospital protocol.

Misoprostol (Cytotec)
Often avoided		Current ACOG VBAC guidance states that misoprostol should not be used for cervical ripening or induction at term after a prior cesarean. Confirm how that guidance applies to this pregnancy.
Other prostaglandins (for example, dinoprostone)
Discuss carefully		Agents and evidence differ, and hospital protocols may be more restrictive than a general guideline. Ask the obstetric team which agents it uses in TOLAC, the estimated risks, and the alternatives.
Mechanical (Foley / Cook)
Ask if appropriate		A transcervical catheter may be an option for some TOLAC candidates with an unfavorable cervix. Suitability depends on the individual situation and unit protocol.
Pitocin (oxytocin)
Discuss carefully		Oxytocin can be considered for induction or augmentation in some TOLACs, but it changes the risk conversation and monitoring plan. There is no app-selected dose or escalation schedule that is appropriate for every patient.

Heads up: Ask the obstetric team to document which methods are acceptable, what clinical trigger would lead to each, how risk is monitored, and what happens if labor arrests while AROM and oxytocin remain declined. Do not use a dose ceiling copied from an app as an individualized prescription.

Often routine

"Epidural decision pending. Offer non-pharmacologic first (position, shower, breathing, counter-pressure) and nitrous oxide if available. Do not offer epidural — I will ask."

Why / what to discuss

A request not to offer an epidural can be written on the room board while leaving the option available on request. Epidural analgesia can be used during TOLAC; ask anesthesia how it changes mobility, fluids, monitoring, and urgent-cesarean preparation.

Heads up: Nitrous oxide availability varies by unit and is not guaranteed. Ask on the tour, not in triage.

VBAC monitoring communication Often routine

"Please explain the VBAC monitoring plan and tell us promptly about concerning changes. We will report new severe pain, bleeding, faintness, breathing trouble, or any sudden change without trying to interpret it ourselves."

Why / what to discuss

Uterine rupture is uncommon but time-sensitive and is assessed by the clinical team using the fetal tracing, maternal signs, labor progress, and symptoms together. A lay symptom list cannot rule it in or out; the useful preference is rapid two-way communication.

2Delivery and pushing4 preferences
Often routine

"Spontaneous, open-glottis pushing on my urge and timing. No directed counting. Any position as tracing permits."

Why / what to discuss

ACOG supports individualized pushing and positions when maternal and fetal status permit. Ask the bedside team to coach only when useful and to explain if the tracing, exhaustion, epidural effects, or an urgent delivery changes the approach.

Heads up: With an epidural, position options narrow to what she can hold safely.

FIRM Often routine

"No episiotomy. Perineal support and warm compresses preferred. I accept natural tearing."

Why / what to discuss

Routine episiotomy is not recommended. Warm compresses and perineal support may be offered during the second stage. Ask the clinician to explain any proposed episiotomy and why it is thought necessary in that moment.

Heads up: An urgent operative delivery can change the risk-benefit assessment. The emergency and consent language at the top already leaves room for that conversation.

Often routine

"Delay cord clamping for at least 30–60 seconds when the baby is vigorous and the clinical situation allows; discuss a longer delay if appropriate."

Why / what to discuss

ACOG recommends delaying clamping for at least 30–60 seconds in vigorous term and preterm infants. A longer delay is a separate preference to discuss; it should not be attributed to ACOG's minimum recommendation.

Often routine

"Cord cutting: my support person, if clinically feasible."

Why / what to discuss

Routine, and usually offered without being asked.

Often routine

"When parent and baby are stable, immediate skin-to-skin with drying and initial observation on my chest. Avoid separation when clinically appropriate; explain first if circumstances allow."

Why / what to discuss

When parent and newborn are stable, many teams can provide drying, stimulation, initial observation, and Apgar scoring during skin-to-skin. Ask what the local unit does at the bedside and what clinical findings would require separation.

Heads up: A baby who needs resuscitation goes to the warmer. That is the emergency clause working as intended, not a broken promise.

Often routine

"Protect the golden hour — defer weighing, measuring, and non-essential procedures 60+ min."

Why / what to discuss

For a stable newborn, routine weighing and measurements can often wait while required assessments and treatments remain on schedule. Ask which care can happen at the bedside and what would require an interruption.

DECIDE Settle prenatally

"Please review newborn erythromycin prophylaxis with us before administration, including its benefits, maternal screening results, Virginia requirements, and the hospital's consent or declination process. Document our final decision."

Why / what to discuss

Erythromycin helps prevent gonococcal eye infection, which can cause severe eye injury. CDC guidance recommends universal neonatal ocular prophylaxis because not every pregnant patient is screened and infection can be acquired after an earlier negative test. A documented negative maternal screen is relevant context, but it does not make the risk zero; bring the result and discuss timing and exposure risk with the pediatric team.

Virginia note: 12VAC5-90-140 directs the attending clinician to ensure erythromycin is administered. It is not an opt-out statute and does not describe a refusal process. Hospital handling of a parental declination may vary, so do not cite this rule as authority to refuse; ask the hospital and pediatrician before delivery.

Often routine

"Delay first bath 24+ hours, or decline entirely (preserving vernix)."

Why / what to discuss

WHO recommends delaying the first bath for at least 24 hours while keeping the newborn warm. Ask the local unit about timing, temperature stability, and any clinical reason to bathe sooner.

Often routine

"Baby stays with me when clinically appropriate; please perform routine assessments in the room. My support person accompanies the baby if transport is needed and policy allows."

Why / what to discuss

Many units support rooming-in and bedside assessments, but capabilities and clinical needs vary. Confirm what can happen in the room and how a support person can accompany the baby if separation is necessary.

Usually yes — ask

"Exclusive breastfeeding. No pacifiers, bottles, or formula without my consent. Lactation consult within 24 hrs."

Why / what to discuss

Feeding-support availability and timing vary by hospital. Ask during prenatal planning how to request lactation help and what coverage is available overnight or on weekends.

Heads up: A clinician may recommend supplementation for low blood sugar, excessive weight loss, dehydration, or another concern. Ask for the indication and feeding plan, and discuss whether expressed colostrum, donor milk, or formula is available and appropriate.

Settle prenatally

"Hepatitis B vaccine (birth dose): pending — confirming HBsAg-negative status. Explicit consent required before administration."

Why / what to discuss

As reviewed July 2026, AAP continues to recommend a universal birth dose within 24 hours for medically stable newborns. CDC's December 2025 guidance calls for an individual shared decision when the mother is confirmed HBsAg-negative, while babies of HBsAg-positive or unknown-status mothers still need time-critical prophylaxis. Ask the pediatrician to explain which schedule they recommend and why.

Heads up: Bring the verified HBsAg result, but do not treat it as the whole decision. Maternal status, household exposure, follow-up reliability, current recommendations, and hospital policy all matter. Record the agreed plan before delivery when possible.

Discuss carefully

"I would like my support person present in the OR, including during spinal or epidural placement, if the anesthesia team and safety policy allow."

Why / what to discuss

The anesthesia team and operating-room policy determine when a support person may enter. Some units wait until neuraxial anesthesia is placed and the sterile field is ready; practices vary.

Heads up: Ask the anesthesia group prenatally, document the answer in the chart, and make a backup support plan in case clinical circumstances or staffing change.

Usually yes — ask

"Clear drape preferred — I want to see baby being born."

Why / what to discuss

Availability and sterile-field rules vary. Ask the hospital in advance whether clear drapes are stocked and whether outside equipment is prohibited.

"Delayed cord clamping >=60 sec if clinically possible."

Why / what to discuss

ACOG recommends delaying cord clamping for at least 30 to 60 seconds for vigorous term and preterm infants. It may also be possible during cesarean birth; ask how the local team handles clinical exceptions.

Usually yes — ask

"Skin-to-skin in the OR when parent and baby are stable (my support person if I cannot), continued in recovery. Keep the baby with us when clinically appropriate; my support person accompanies the baby if separated and policy allows."

Why / what to discuss

Operating-room skin-to-skin depends on parent and newborn stability, team workflow, and available staff. Prenatal planning gives the unit time to explain its process and alternatives.

Heads up: Confirm prenatally whether the hospital supports OR skin-to-skin and what staffing is required, then put the preference in the chart.
