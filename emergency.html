Whoever you call, whoever picks up, say this first:

"I gave birth on [date]."

Serious pregnancy-related complications can happen during pregnancy and for up to a year after delivery. Tell the 911 operator, emergency desk, and every clinician that you recently gave birth.

POST · call 911 now

Call 911

AWHONN's post-birth warning signs. These apply for a full year after birth.

PPain in the chest

A blood clot in the lung, or a heart problem. It is not normal to have chest pain after having a baby.

OObstructed breathing or shortness of breath

Same causes. Do not talk yourself out of this one.

SSeizures

Can mean eclampsia: blood pressure high enough to cause a seizure. It happens after birth, not only before.

TThoughts of hurting yourself or the baby

A medical emergency, and a treatable one. Call 911 or 988. You are not a bad mother for having the thought.

BIRTH · call your provider now

Do not wait for office hours. If you cannot reach anyone, go to the ER.

BBleeding: a pad an hour, or egg-size clots

Soaking through more than one pad in an hour, or clots the size of an egg or bigger, or passing tissue. This is obstetric hemorrhage.

IIncision that is not healing

Any cesarean or tear repair that opens, drains, or reddens.

RRed or swollen leg, painful or warm to the touch

A blood clot in the leg. Do not massage it. Call.

TTemperature of 100.4 F or higher

Or chills. Infection.

HHeadache that will not go away

Especially unrelieved by medicine, or with vision changes. Postpartum high blood pressure, which can arrive days or weeks after birth.

SSevere swelling of hands or face

Sudden or extreme swelling—especially with headache, vision changes, trouble breathing, or upper-abdominal pain—can be a warning sign of a blood-pressure complication. Call now.

VBAC and after a cesarean

During labor: uterine rupture — tell the nurse now

Uterine rupture shows as: severe persistent scar pain, a sudden change in the quality of the pain, blood in the urine, the baby's head receding, an abnormal heart tracing, maternal tachycardia, contractions falling apart, or sudden vaginal bleeding. Tell the nurse immediately.

After a cesarean: watch the incision

The incision is the thing to watch: opening, draining, spreading redness, or a fever with it.

Your mind counts too

Baby blues vs. postpartum depression — the two-week line

Weepy and overwhelmed in the first two weeks is usually the baby blues. Call your provider if, past two weeks, sadness or dread won't lift, you can't sleep even when the baby does, you lose interest in the baby, or anger feels out of character — postpartum depression and anxiety affect about 1 in 7 and are treatable. Get medical help now

Young infants can become seriously ill quickly, and early signs may be subtle.

Fever: 100.4 °F (38 °C)+ rectal — call immediately

For a baby under 3 months, a rectal temperature of 100.4 F (38 C) or higher needs immediate medical guidance and prompt evaluation. Call the pediatrician or on-call service now and follow where they direct you to be seen. If the baby looks seriously ill, has breathing trouble, is hard to wake, or you cannot reach clinical help promptly, use emergency care. Do not give fever medicine unless a clinician tells you to.

Breathing trouble: grunting, flaring, blue lips

Fast breathing, grunting with each breath, nostrils flaring, skin sucking in between the ribs, or blue or gray lips, skin, or nails.

Lethargy: hard to wake, floppy

Hard to wake, unusually floppy, weak, or not interacting. If the baby does not respond normally to gentle stimulation, seek urgent medical help.

Color change: pale, mottled, gray, or blue

Pale, mottled, gray, or blue.

Inconsolable, high-pitched crying

That cannot be soothed at all.

Call the pediatrician today

Feeding refusal or missed feeds

New difficulty feeding, repeated refusal, too sleepy to stay awake for feeds, or substantially fewer feeds than the baby's care plan. Breastfeeding newborns commonly feed 8 to 12 times in 24 hours, but formula, combination feeding, prematurity, and individual plans differ.

Too few wet diapers (under 6 a day)

After the first week, fewer than 6 wet diapers in 24 hours, or no wet diaper at all for 6 to 8 hours. The tracker counts this for you and warns.

Not back to birth weight by 2 weeks

Babies lose weight at first and should regain it by about two weeks.

Jaundice: yellow skin or eyes

Yellow skin or eyes. Usually shows up day 2 to 4. Yellow within the first 24 hours is urgent.

Vomiting: forceful, green, or bloody

Repeated forceful or projectile vomiting needs a prompt call. Green vomit, blood, severe pain, a swollen belly, unusual sleepiness, or signs of dehydration need urgent medical assessment now.

Dehydration: dry mouth, sunken eyes or soft spot

Very dry mouth, sunken eyes, a sunken soft spot, unusual sleepiness, or a clear drop in wet diapers. Young newborns may not make visible tears normally, so tears alone are not a reliable test.

Before you call: the 5 things to have ready

The temperature and exactly when you took it, how it was taken (rectal), any medicine given with dose and time, when the last feed was, and the wet and dirty diaper count for the last 24 hours. The tracker has the last three.

Most of this is normal

The red flags are above. These common newborn things look alarming and usually aren't — but trust your gut, and call if something feels wrong. Tap a topic to open it.

Cluster feeding & the witching hour

Evenings of near-constant feeding and fussiness — especially weeks 2 to 6 — are normal and often peak around 6 weeks. It's building your supply and winding them down, not a sign you're doing anything wrong.

Call if: the wet diapers drop off (fewer than 6 a day after the first week, or none for 6–8 hours), or they truly can't be consoled and seem sick rather than fussy.

Funny breathing, hiccups & snorts

Newborn breathing can be irregular, with short pauses followed by faster breaths. Hiccups, sneezes, and some snorty sounds are common, but watch the baby's color, alertness, and effort—not only a timer.

Call 911 if: the baby does not resume breathing promptly, turns blue or gray, becomes limp or unresponsive, or has severe work of breathing. Grunting with every breath or skin pulling in between the ribs needs urgent medical assessment.

Newborn looks: spots, peeling, crossed eyes

Peeling skin, baby acne, tiny white bumps (milia), cradle cap, a cone-shaped head, puffy eyes, and eyes that cross or wander in the early weeks are all normal. So are swollen genitals, tiny breast buds, and even a little vaginal spotting or discharge in girls in the first days — that's mom's hormones clearing.

Diapers that surprise you — color guide inside

The first days bring black tarry meconium, then green, then yellow and seedy (breast) or tan and firmer (formula). A brick-dust orange stain in the first few days is usually concentrated urine, not blood.

Call if: that orange stain appears after day 4–5 (a feeding check), or you see white or chalky stool, red or bloody stool, or black stool after the meconium is long gone.

Loud sleep, startles & spit-up

Newborns are noisy sleepers — grunts, squeaks, and groans — and sleep 14 to 17 hours in scattered chunks. The sudden arms-out startle (the Moro reflex) is normal, and so are small, effortless spit-ups. Always place them on their back to sleep, every time.

Call if: spit-up turns forceful or projectile, is green, or comes after every feed.

Losing weight at first

Babies normally lose up to about 7–10% of their birth weight in the first days, then climb back to it by around two weeks. Your pediatrician tracks this at the early visits.
