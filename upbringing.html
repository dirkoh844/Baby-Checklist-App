Recovery & the fourth trimester

weeks 0–8

The weeks right after birth belong to healing and bonding. Newborns are especially vulnerable to everyday germs for roughly the first 6–8 weeks, so setting visitor expectations before the birth protects both the baby and your energy. Framing a boundary as a plan rather than a rejection keeps the peace:

"We're keeping the first weeks quiet to rest and settle in. Can we book a visit for the weekend of [date]?"

Mom's body is navigating hormonal shifts, uterine involution, physical trauma recovery, and possibly C-section aftercare all at once. Healing unfolds on its own schedule; planning for that ahead of time (meals, help, low expectations) protects mental health too. The Labor page covers the when-to-call red flags.

The first 30 days

sleep & feeding

Sleep is real but shattered. Newborns total 11–17 hours a day in bursts of 30 minutes to 3 hours, with awake windows rarely past 2 hours. That fragmentation is normal physiology (their circadian rhythm barely exists yet); true regular cycles don't solidify until around 6 months. Sleep when the baby sleeps is advice, not a cliché.

Feed on cues, not the clock. The first feed usually happens within 30–60 minutes of delivery, delivering early immune protection and heading off low blood sugar. After that, watch for early hunger cues: rooting, nuzzling toward the breast, hands to mouth. Crying is a late hunger cue; catching the earlier ones makes latching and settling far easier.

The Reminders page can nudge water and vitamins while you're running on newborn time.

1–6 months: tummy time & milestones

motor development

Daily tummy time builds the neck, chest, and core strength behind every later milestone and helps prevent flat spots. Always awake and supervised:

AgeDaily amountHow
0–4 wk~20 min in 3–5 min micro-sessionsOn your chest, across the lap, or a firm clean blanket
1–3 mo20–30 min in 5–10 min sessionsFlat, safe floor surface such as a play mat
4–5 moBuild up to ~60 minAdd toys to encourage reaching, rolling, and pivoting

By around six months, many babies roll tummy-to-back, push up on straight arms, and lean on their hands while sitting. Socially, expect squeals, real laughs, raspberries, and back-and-forth "conversations." Everything goes in the mouth from here on, so start choking-hazard vigilance now, and keep the vaccination schedule on track at every well visit.

6–12 months: solids & babyproofing

big transitions

The AAP and the Dietary Guidelines both point to starting solids at about 6 months, but readiness matters more than the calendar:

Readiness signWhy it matters
Strong head & neck controlSits in a high chair with steady head control, keeping the airway safe
Tongue-thrust reflex fadingFood moves back to be swallowed instead of being pushed straight out
Roughly doubled birth weightAround 13 lb or more, when the gut can handle solids

Rotate variety early: oat, barley, and multigrain cereals rather than rice-only (which limits inorganic arsenic exposure), plus purees and soft textures. Breast milk or formula stays the primary nutrition source through the first birthday. Sleep consolidates to 12–16 hour totals with 6+ hour night stretches for many babies.

Mobility means babyproofing, now: clear small swallowable objects, keep hot liquids out of reach, stay rear-facing in the car seat, and hold off on screens (video chat is the exception).

Drawn from AAP, CDC, and Nemours KidsHealth guidance. Educational reference, not medical advice — your pediatrician's instructions always come first.
