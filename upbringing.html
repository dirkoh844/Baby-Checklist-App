Recovery & the fourth trimester

weeks 0–8

The weeks right after birth belong to healing and bonding. Tap a topic to open it.

Visitors: why the first 6–8 weeks stay quiet (+ a script)

Newborns are especially vulnerable to everyday germs for roughly the first 6–8 weeks, so setting visitor expectations before the birth protects both the baby and your energy. Framing a boundary as a plan rather than a rejection keeps the peace:

"We're keeping the first weeks quiet to rest and settle in. Can we book a visit for the weekend of [date]?"

What mom's body is healing from

Hormonal shifts, uterine involution, physical trauma recovery, and possibly C-section aftercare all at once. Healing unfolds on its own schedule; planning for that ahead of time (meals, help, low expectations) protects mental health too. The Labor page covers the when-to-call red flags.

How you're really doing

your mind

Your body isn't the only thing recovering. Big feelings after birth are normal and expected — this is here to help you tell an ordinary hard day from something worth a call. None of it is a diagnosis, and none of it is your fault. Tap a topic to open it.

Baby blues, or something more?

Baby blues hit up to 80% of new parents: weepy, overwhelmed, moody, running hot and cold. They usually peak around days 3–5 and ease by about two weeks as hormones settle.

If it lasts longer than two weeks, gets heavier instead of lighter, or steals the good moments, it may be postpartum depression or anxiety — which affect about 1 in 7 and are very treatable. It can begin any time in the first year, not just right after birth.

Call your provider if…

Reach out to your OB, midwife, or doctor if — most days, for more than two weeks — you notice: sadness or emptiness that won't lift; crying you can't explain; no interest in things you used to enjoy, or in the baby; can't sleep even when the baby sleeps, or sleeping all the time; constant dread, racing thoughts, or a pounding heart; anger or irritability that feels out of character; or feeling like a failure, or that your family would be better off without you.

You don't have to be at your worst to call. A quick questionnaire at your visit can sort it out, and treatment works.

For partners & support people

You often see it first. Watch for withdrawal, hopelessness, not eating or sleeping, rage, or frightening comments — and take it seriously rather than waiting it out. Ask directly ("how are you really doing?"), take a night feed, book the appointment, and go along to it.

Partners get this too — roughly 1 in 10 non-birthing parents experience postpartum depression. This is for you as well.

The first 30 days

sleep & feeding

The ABCs of safe sleep — every sleep, day and night

Newborn sleep: short, irregular stretches are normal

Many newborns sleep about 14–17 hours across 24 hours, split into short stretches, but individual patterns vary. Day-night rhythms develop gradually. Protect at least one caregiver rest block when possible, and call the pediatrician if the baby is unusually hard to wake or cannot stay awake to feed.

Feed on cues, not the clock — the early hunger signs

For a stable parent and baby, skin-to-skin and support for the first feed are commonly offered soon after birth. After that, watch for early hunger cues: rooting, nuzzling, stirring, and hands to mouth. Crying is a later cue; feeding support and any medical plan from the newborn team take priority.

Stomach size: cherry → walnut → egg

A newborn's stomach is tiny — tiny feeds and constant hunger are the design, not a supply problem.

How to swaddle — the 4 folds

Snug at the arms, loose at the hips — legs should bend freely. Always place a swaddled baby on the back, keep loose bedding out, and stop swaddling at the first sign of trying to roll; this can happen earlier than expected.

Burping — 3 holds that work

Burp mid-feed and after. A few minutes of gentle pats is plenty — some feeds just don't produce one, and that's fine.

Birth–6 months: tummy time & milestones

motor development

Daily tummy time builds the neck, chest, and core strength behind every later milestone and helps prevent flat spots. Always awake and supervised:

AgeDaily amountHow
Newborn2–3 sessions of 3–5 minStart soon after coming home; chest or lap time counts while fully awake and watched
By ~7 wkWork toward 15–30 min totalUse several short sessions on a firm, clear floor surface
Older babyKeep adding time as toleratedAdd floor play and toys; stop if sleepy or distressed and try again later
What 6-month milestones look like

By around six months, many babies roll tummy-to-back, push up on straight arms, and lean on their hands while sitting. Socially, expect squeals, real laughs, raspberries, and back-and-forth "conversations." Everything goes in the mouth from here on, so start choking-hazard vigilance now, and keep the vaccination schedule on track at every well visit.

6–12 months: solids & babyproofing

big transitions

The AAP and the Dietary Guidelines both point to starting solids at about 6 months, but readiness matters more than the calendar:

Readiness signWhy it matters
Strong head & neck controlSits in a high chair with steady head control, keeping the airway safe
Tongue-thrust reflex fadingFood moves back to be swallowed instead of being pushed straight out
Reaches for food and brings objects to mouthShows interest and the coordination needed to explore food with support
First foods: variety beats rice-only

Rotate variety early: oat, barley, and multigrain cereals rather than rice-only (which limits inorganic arsenic exposure), plus purees and soft textures. Breast milk or formula stays the primary nutrition source through the first birthday. Sleep consolidates to 12–16 hour totals with 6+ hour night stretches for many babies.

Mobility = babyproofing, now — the 4 rules

Clear small swallowable objects, keep hot liquids out of reach, stay rear-facing in the car seat, and hold off on screens (video chat is the exception).
